Preface

This is the first in a series of e-books that begin talking of the Barefoot experience. I hope it can capture the issues, the colour, energy, laughter and joy of working together. Community work is not a chore. Sharing within a collective, as equals, brings extraordinary learning. I hope you are able to share this e-book as well.

I have always wanted to make my ideas work. But I also know that they are not just my ideas, ever. They are shared ideas. These 58 years of work, mainly with poor people, have taught me

how important it is to listen. Our stories, if heard or seen, may make a difference. This e-book is a result of many stories and many journeys, as I went from being a committed social worker in the early 1970s looking at integrated development in Ajmer district of Rajasthan, to being a consultant in the Planning Commission, when I traveled extensively all over India and, later, all over the world. I went to remote villages in over 90 countries, to identify village women who could come and be trained in Tilonia as Barefoot Solar Engineers. These journeys have always been with communities and with people who are committed to similar ideas and ideals.

Barefoot College, Tilonia – home to me and to my colleagues – has provided space for people to learn from the underprivileged and

the poor. You do not need a degree to be a professional, or a university education to make policy. It has been an exciting journey full of learning, successes and failures. In Tilonia, we value failure, and see it as a great opportunity for education and growth. It makes us work till we find a solution, despite adverse opinion. You keep going. As Mahatma Gandhi said: “First they ignore you, then they laugh at you, then they fight you, then you win.”

We all know that rural India and the rural world make up a large percentage of the global population. And yet they have to fight for resources and recognition. It is time we bridged this gap.

This e-book is for you to read, listen to and see. I hope people’s lives inspire you to discover new possibilities with them.

Our journey can be your journey.
Bunker Roy

ORIGINS

When I made the unusual choice to go to Bihar during one of its worst famines in 1966, my life turned upside down. I decided to work and live in a village. Without regret, I left everything behind – the Doon School, St. Stephens, and a promising career as India’s three-time national squash champion.

For the first two years I worked with a drilling team, deepening wells and shocking my conventional family in the process – my mother most of all. From this work I learnt in a very practical way to understand the dangers that workers face – undertaking the perilous task of sitting in a basket and being lowered into a well, to set dynamite that would blast for water. Learning by doing, indeed.

In 1972, after many small experiments in Ajmer in the District of Rajasthan, I came to a village – Tilonia – with Meghraj, a fellow driller. In that same year we went to look at an abandoned Government campus, took it on rent and set up the Barefoot College, then called SWRC (Social Work and Research Centre).

The conviction that drove me, and remains my message, is that you have to listen to the local people. They know what their problems are. They know the solutions, too, and we must support them to shape these ideas and bring them to fruition.

The College worked with, and for, the poor. I realised from the start that people needed to own and steer the institution. Support came from

In arid Rajasthan, the constant, and principal cry was: “WATER!” Working with unlettered, ordinary people, the Barefoot College team began by identifying new water sources through surveys, as well as experimenting with ways to recharge open wells and hand pumps. The College then went on to share engineering skills with local people, revive the ancient practices of rainwater harvesting and set up reverse osmosis plants.

Many challenges sometimes needed crazy solutions! And some of those just didn’t work – such as our attempt to make our own windmills for pumping up water. But in tackling them people learnt from their outcomes. In the Barefoot philosophy, there was no such word as failure.

From water divining to setting up handpumps, untrained village men, and later women, learnt technical skills, becoming water-warriors and hand pump mistris. The process was natural, logical and self-sustaining.

WATER: CULTURAL SETTING-1

Fabled Rajasthan is rich in colour, kings, palaces and temples, history and tradition. It is also a land of climate extremes, and is India’s driest state. From May to August the relentless sun bakes soil and people alike. From June to September, monsoons give relief and restore life to parched crops.

But the rains do not always come. Drought brings famine, death and fear. But hard as life can be, for thousands of years monsoons have replenished underground water, enough to sustain life even through a period of sustained drought.

To desert dwellers water is a priceless treasure, and the people of Rajasthan have always revered it.

Traditional songs about water echo across the land. Water was what our forefathers looked for when they chose to settle, by a river or a lake. Some people say that in past geological times Rajasthan was a sea – a sea which has left fossils. Not only does it mean life itself, but water – especially clean, pure, potable water – is rare, and traditional ways of collecting it have increasing problems.

In the early 70s, water was a problem for the whole community, and very high on the priority list. For those who came from urban India, going to a village was like stepping back in a time machine. It was said it’s easier to drink milk than water, which was far harder to get. Women walked miles to fetch water – for drinking, bathing, cooking and for the animals. Building a trough for animals and making a ‘piao’, a water point managed by the upper caste for humans, were crucially important and highly valued actions for piety and charity.

The lack of water had an enormous impact on health and hygiene, and children’s diseases abounded. In country villages infant deaths were often thought to be caused by witches’ curses, rather than water that was unsafe to drink whether through excess fluorides or other chemicals, or having been contaminated by animals. The water table dropped drastically from 60 feet to over 300 feet over the years.

WATER: TECHNOLOGY-1

At the time we started working with water, in theory rural villages had access to it through established government systems that included traditional wells and open sources. In practice there were real difficulties with these. Open water was shared with animals, so it was often contaminated. The women’s work (because it was always the women who had the job of fetching water) often involved pulling water out with a rope from deep wells into pots. It was arduous and exhausting.

From the beginning, Barefoot College made it a central and urgent priority to tackle the immense problem of getting potable drinking water to the poor, and to far-flung rural desert communities. Over the years Barefoot College experimented with what others thought were crazy ideas. We began with geological technical surveys, the use of which gained the approval of the Rajasthan Ground Water Board.

We tried and tested new and different methods of open well and hand pump recharge, including UNICEF’s Three-Tier System. But always, conversations with people with abundant knowledge remained a focus for understanding traditional methods of water conservation. We had great respect for the competence of local people and worked hard to demystify technology to make it accessible to them.

Our beliefs were vindicated when, in the face of problems with the Three-Tier System, we transferred engineering skills to local people. This resulted in the Hand Pump Mistri or HPM, and was very successful.

Throughout the 1980s and 1990s Rajasthan continued to adopt Barefoot College’s Hand Pump Mistri local maintenance scheme. It generated employment among young village people, gave them self-respect and freed villages of at least one branch of government bureaucracy. It also dealt with gender, caste and untouchability issues, as many of the mistris were women and Dalits.

WATER: IMPACT-1

When we started this journey, we knew it would test our staying power, commitment and ingenuity. We had no strategic plan but grew organically, always responsive to the ideas and strengths of a people who depended on the effectiveness of their ideas if they were to survive and meet their own basic human needs.

At first water, food and shelter were the most pressing of these. But slowly, as people made the connection between illness and contaminated water, their needs shifted from ‘water’ to ‘safe drinking water’.

When you work by solving problems as they arise, a picture evolves, and you begin to see how tightly survival, technology and development are linked and interconnected with many aspects of people’s lives. This understanding is a great part of the Barefoot College legacy.

This ebook has traced the making of that legacy, which has taken us through a broad spectrum of activities. They have included drilling for groundwater, installing and maintaining handpumps, constructing and deepening village ponds and dams, exploring ways to rejuvenate existing wells and open water sources, making the most of rainwater harvesting, and constructing reverse osmosis plants in fluoride and arsenic-affected areas.

It’s been a huge criss-cross of ideas and work with communities. What began with one set of villages surrounding the Tilonia campus spread beyond Rajasthan into other Indian states, and later moved beyond India into many countries in the Global South, especially in Africa, Latin America, and Southeast Asia.

Thanks to our diligent barefoot record-keepers, counting the numbers has been easy and exciting. Watch Jokhim Chacha, the Barefoot College mascot, lead you through its narration. It may sound impressive to say that we have installed 1735 handpumps in Indian villages, or 27 community rainwater harvesting tanks in seven other countries.

Figures speak their own testimony. But as impressive as it may sound, ‘tangible achievements’ are easy to measure and demonstrate.

But figures are only part of the story. The rest is told in the lives of the people we set out to help, the ones who live and struggle for survival every day.

Barefoot College assesses change by the improvement in people’s lives, by seeing people’s self-confidence rise, by empowerment being enabled and expectations being changed.

In practice, all markers of improvement matter. Barefoot leaves its footprint in the visible, the tangible and in the improved quality of life that people acknowledge with articulation and confidence.

But the impact of the numbers of people we have trained as water engineers, and the numbers of women who have become community leaders, speaks more loudly than the numbers of hand-pumps we have installed.
